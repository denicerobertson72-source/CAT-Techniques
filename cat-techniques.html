Focused Listing

Chapter 7 – Subject Matter Learning
Overview: Students generate a quick list of key terms/ideas in response to a focused prompt.
Purpose: Reveal what students recall and how they organize core content; surface gaps and misconceptions.
In-class: ≤5 minutes
Out-of-class: 0 minutes
Prep: Minimal
Review: Quick Scan
Implementation Steps:
  • Provide a focused prompt tied to a single concept/topic.
  • Give 2–5 minutes for individual listing.
  • Collect responses and scan for patterns (missing essentials, misconceptions).
  • Close the loop by addressing themes and adjusting instruction.
Student Prompt Examples:
  • List the most important ideas related to [TOPIC].
  • List the steps/components of [PROCESS].
Materials:
  • Prompt (slide/handout)
Variations:
  • Pair-and-merge lists; compare to an expert list.
  • Use at start (diagnostic) or end (review).
Tips:
  • Look for patterns rather than grading individuals.
  • Respond quickly so students see value.
AI Adaptation Notes:
  • Use AI to draft alternative prompts at different cognitive levels.
  • Use AI to cluster anonymized responses into themes (remove identifiers first).

Memory Matrix

Chapter 7 – Subject Matter Learning
Overview: Students complete a partially filled matrix that displays relationships among concepts.
Purpose: Assess structured understanding and differentiation across dimensions.
In-class: 10–15 minutes
Out-of-class: 0–15 minutes
Prep: Moderate
Review: Moderate
Implementation Steps:
  • Choose 3–6 row categories and 3–6 column dimensions.
  • Create a blank matrix (optionally with a word bank).
  • Students fill it out individually or in pairs.
  • Review for systematic confusions; debrief with an expert model.
Student Prompt Examples:
  • Complete the matrix comparing [ROW CATEGORIES] across [COLUMN DIMENSIONS].
Materials:
  • Prepared matrix (paper or digital)
Variations:
  • Provide a partial "skeleton" matrix for novices.
  • Use pre/post to show conceptual growth.
Tips:
  • Keep dimensions meaningful and unambiguous.
  • Pilot quickly with a colleague or TA.
AI Adaptation Notes:
  • Use AI to propose row/column dimensions aligned to outcomes.
  • Use AI to draft an instructor key + likely misconceptions per cell.
AI Prompt for Faculty Prep 
Create a Memory Matrix classroom assessment.

Course: [COURSE NAME]
Topic/Unit: [TOPIC]
Learning objectives:
- [OBJECTIVE 1]
- [OBJECTIVE 2]

Propose:
1) 4–6 ROW categories and 4–6 COLUMN dimensions aligned to the objectives.
2) A student-facing blank matrix (markdown table) with concise instructions.
3) An instructor key (completed matrix).
4) 5 misconception patterns this matrix may reveal and what I should do if I see each.
Keep each cell to 3–8 words.

Background Knowledge Probe

Chapter 7 – Subject Matter Learning
Overview: A short, focused diagnostic questionnaire administered before instruction.
Purpose: Identify prior knowledge and misconceptions to select the best starting point.
In-class: ≤10 minutes
Out-of-class: 0–10 minutes
Prep: Moderate
Review: Quick Scan
Implementation Steps:
  • Draft 3–7 high-leverage questions for the upcoming unit.
  • Administer at the start of the unit (low stakes).
  • Scan results for misconceptions and uneven preparation.
  • Adjust instruction and tell students what you changed based on results.
Student Prompt Examples:
  • In 1–2 sentences: What do you already know about [TOPIC]?
  • Define [KEY TERM] in your own words.
Materials:
  • Short questionnaire (paper, form, or LMS quiz)
Variations:
  • Add a confidence rating to each answer.
  • Re-administer later as a post-probe.
Tips:
  • Target common misconceptions.
  • Keep it brief and clearly instructional (not evaluative).
AI Adaptation Notes:
  • Use AI to draft probe items aligned to unit objectives and common misconceptions.
  • Use AI to suggest instructional responses for result patterns.
AI Prompt for Faculty Prep 
Design a Background Knowledge Probe.

Course: [COURSE NAME]
Unit/Topic: [UNIT/TOPIC]
Student level: [INTRO/INTERMEDIATE/ADVANCED]
Key objectives:
- [OBJECTIVE 1]
- [OBJECTIVE 2]

Create 6 short questions (mix: definition, example, misconception check, application).
For each question, include:
- What it diagnoses
- What I should do instructionally if many students struggle.
Keep answers short (1–3 minutes each).

Directed Paraphrasing

Chapter 7 – Subject Matter Learning
Overview: Students explain a concept in their own words for a specified audience.
Purpose: Assess depth of understanding and ability to translate knowledge.
In-class: 10–15 minutes
Out-of-class: 0–15 minutes
Prep: Moderate
Review: Moderate
Implementation Steps:
  • Choose a key concept/process.
  • Specify the target audience and constraints (length, tone).
  • Students write a short paraphrase; you review for accuracy and audience fit.
  • Share exemplars and address common confusions.
Student Prompt Examples:
  • Explain [CONCEPT] to a [TARGET AUDIENCE] in [LENGTH].
Materials:
  • Prompt with audience specification
Variations:
  • Use two audiences (novice vs expert) and compare.
  • Peer review for clarity.
Tips:
  • Pick audiences students can imagine concretely.
  • Score meaning over style.
AI Adaptation Notes:
  • Use AI to propose audiences and constraints.
  • Use AI to draft a simple rubric and "look-fors" checklist.

Target audience: [AUDIENCE]
Length: [WORD COUNT or TIME LIMIT]

- [IDEA 1]
- [IDEA 2]

Produce:
1) Student-facing prompt with constraints.
2) A 3-criterion rubric (Accuracy, Clarity, Audience Fit) with 3 performance levels.
3) 5 common errors/misconceptions + quick feedback phrases.

Documented Problem-Set Solutions

Chapter 7 – Subject Matter Learning
Overview: Students document each step in solving a problem, including reasoning and checks.
Purpose: Make thinking visible and diagnose where reasoning breaks down.
In-class: 15–30 minutes
Out-of-class: 0–30 minutes
Prep: Moderate
Review: Deep Analysis
Implementation Steps:
  • Select a representative problem.
  • Provide a template requiring "What I did" and "Why" for each step.
  • Students solve and annotate their process.
  • Review for breakdowns (setup, strategy, execution, checking) and debrief with an expert model.
Student Prompt Examples:
  • Solve [PROBLEM]. For each step, write what you did and why.
  • Describe how you checked your answer.
Materials:
  • Problem statement
  • Documentation template
Variations:
  • Ask students to mark uncertainty points.
  • Compare two solution paths and justify.
Tips:
  • Choose problems that elicit common errors.
  • Look for "silent leaps" without justification.
AI Adaptation Notes:
  • Use AI to draft an "expert reasoning" key for instructor reference.
  • Use AI to anticipate common errors and feedback comments.
AI Prompt for Faculty Prep 
Create a Documented Problem-Set Solutions template.

Course: [COURSE NAME]
Problem type: [TYPE]
Problem (paste): [PASTE PROBLEM]
Objective: [OBJECTIVE]

Generate:
1) Student worksheet sections: Setup, Strategy, Step-by-step (What/Why), Check/Validate, Reflection.
2) Instructor key showing expert reasoning.
3) 6 common reasoning errors + short feedback phrase for each.

Do & Say (Function & Content) Analysis

Chapter 7 – Critical Thinking & Analysis
Overview: Students analyze what an author/speaker is doing (function) and what they are saying (content).
Purpose: Assess rhetorical/structural analysis and comprehension of meaning.
In-class: 15–25 minutes
Out-of-class: 0–20 minutes
Prep: Moderate
Review: Deep Analysis
Implementation Steps:
  • Select a short text/excerpt or lecture segment.
  • Provide a two-column DO/SAY template and model one line.
  • Students complete; discuss how function shapes meaning.
Student Prompt Examples:
  • DO: name the rhetorical move. SAY: paraphrase the content in your own words.
Materials:
  • Excerpt
  • Two-column template
Variations:
  • Add a third column: "So what?" implications.
  • Jigsaw different segments then share.
Tips:
  • Start with short excerpts.
  • Provide a function-verb bank for novices.
AI Adaptation Notes:
  • Use AI to generate a discipline-appropriate function-verb bank.
  • Use AI to draft an instructor modeling example (first 1–2 rows).
AI Prompt for Faculty Prep 
Create a Do & Say Analysis handout.

Course: [COURSE NAME]
Excerpt (paste): [PASTE EXCERPT]
Student level: [LEVEL]

Generate:
1) A DO/SAY template with 6–10 rows.
2) A function-verb bank (10–15 verbs) appropriate to the excerpt.
3) A completed instructor example for 2 rows.
4) 5 debrief questions that move from identifying moves to evaluating effectiveness.

Focused Dialectical Notes

Chapter 7 – Critical Thinking & Analysis
Overview: Students keep structured notes separating key ideas from reactions/questions.
Purpose: Support critical engagement; assess interpretation and response quality.
In-class: 5–15 minutes
Out-of-class: 10–30 minutes
Prep: Moderate
Review: Moderate
Implementation Steps:
  • Provide a two-column Ideas/Reactions format.
  • Students complete during/after reading/lecture.
  • Sample and review for patterns; debrief common questions and strong reactions.
Student Prompt Examples:
  • Left: claim/evidence. Right: your reaction (question, critique, connection).
Materials:
  • Dialectical notes template
Variations:
  • Require one connection to a prior concept.
  • Peer exchange notes and add questions.
Tips:
  • Model what counts as a strong reaction (beyond "I agree").
  • Use selective sampling to manage workload.
AI Adaptation Notes:
  • Use AI to draft reaction stems tailored to the topic.
  • Use AI to create a short "quality example" (for instructor modeling).
